instructions:
  - steps:
      - Add the butter to a heavy bottom deep pot and begin to melt on a medium flame.
      - The butter melts and turns frothy. Lower the flame and simmer until most of the froth evaporates.
      - The butter will begin to bubble a lot. Stir it occasionally and continue to cook on a low heat.
      - Soon the butter changes its color from yellow to clear golden. At this stage milk solids will settle at the bottom, and begin to turn slightly light golden-brown. The bubbles will also reduce significantly indicating it is done. Be careful not to let it burn — turn off the heat!
      - The ghee will continue to cook on its own for another 1–2 mins since the pot is hot. By then the ghee should be perfectly done – clear, golden in color and all the milk solids browned but not burnt. If there is still some foam on top, remove it with a spoon.
      - When the temperature comes down, place a strainer over a clean jar and filter it. Press down the brown solids with a spoon.
      - Cool the ghee completely and cover it with a tight lid. The ghee will solidify in a few hours or days depending on the temperature.
      - Store at room temperature in a dry cabinet for up to 3 months or refrigerate for a year.

Ghee has been produced on the Indian subcontinent since ancient times and is deeply woven into the region's culinary and cultural traditions. Statistics estimate that about 30 % of milk is converted into ghee in India. It is used in sweets, as a frying medium, and even as a spread or topping. Today, ghee also has become increasingly popular around the world.

Making ghee or clarified butter is surprisingly simple. Butter is gently simmered until its water evaporates and the milk solids sink to the bottom of the pan, where they caramelize. The clear golden fat is then strained, producing a butterfat with a rich, nutty flavor and aroma.

Because the water and milk solids have been removed, ghee keeps much longer than butter and can be stored at room temperature for several months. It also has a much higher smoke point, making it ideal for high-heat cooking while adding a distinctive depth of flavor to both savory and sweet dishes.
