instructions:
  - title: Prepare the pesto
    steps:
      - Note that you can also use nettles, carrot tops, radish greens or other wild herbs as a base instead of basil leaves. The same goes for pine nuts that can be replaced with any kind of seeds or nuts you have on hand.
      - Soak your basil leaves in cold water for 10 minutes. This optional step slows down the leaves oxidation, avoiding bitterness.
      - Tare your food processor, add the pine nuts and blend until the nuts are very finely chopped.
      - Lift the basil from the bowl of water, shake the excess water off, and add it to the food processor. Check the combine weight of pine nuts and leaves.
      - Add 2–2.5% of the weight of your ingredients in salt. For example, for 400 g of leaves and pine nuts, add approximately 10 g of salt (when in doubt, always round up your salt quantity).
      - Lightly blend, and let it sit for about 15 minutes.
      - Some brine should have been released from your leaves. Transfer everything to a tightly sealed jar. Use a weight to keep the greens underwater.
  - title: Store the pesto
    steps:
      - Let it ferment in a dark cupboard for 3–7 days, and then in a cold room for 3 more weeks — or depending on the sourness you want to achieve. It will keep for months in the refrigerator.
      - Before serving, you can mix in some olive oil, Parmesan cheese, garlic, or anything your pesto might be lacking.

Pesto as we know it today originated in Genoa, where _pesto alla Genovese_ became a regional specialty during the 19th century. The name comes from the Italian verb _pestare_, meaning "to pound," referring to the traditional mortar and pestle used to prepare the sauce.

While delicious, this traditional pesto recipe doesn't keep well for long because of its fresh ingredients.

Fermented pesto takes a different approach. Instead of preserving the herbs in oil, the basil is mixed with salt and left to undergo lactic acid fermentation. Over the following weeks, naturally occurring lactic acid bacteria acidify the mixture, creating an environment that safely preserves the herbs while adding a subtle tanginess.

Once fermentation is complete, the herb paste can be stored in the refrigerator for months. Olive oil, Parmesan, garlic, or other seasonings are only added just before serving, giving you a fresh-tasting pesto whenever you need it.
